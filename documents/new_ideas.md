# CTLS — Next Step Ideas
### Synthesized from Research Discussion, March 2026

---

* **Soft-Weighted Positive Pairs:**
The current positive pair definition (same class label = positive pair) is noisy — the patching validation showed ~50% of same-class pairs don't actually share circuits. Applying a hard attractive force to those pairs distorts the network's natural circuit structure. The fix is to weight the InfoNCE loss by current z-similarity during training rather than filtering by class label alone. High z-similarity same-class pairs attract strongly; low z-similarity same-class pairs get near-zero weight. The multiple valid circuits per class (the "circuit family") then emerge as distinct sub-clusters naturally without being forced to merge. Start with flat weights (standard InfoNCE behavior) and anneal a sharpening temperature in gradually as z organizes. Experiment: train with soft-weighted InfoNCE, compare within-class proxy Spearman ρ against the patching ground truth vs. the current hard class-label version. The predicted outcome is higher within-class ρ and visible sub-cluster structure in UMAP.

* **Final-Layer Baseline Ablation:**
The entire trajectory apparatus (multi-layer projection, depth-aware combination, meta-encoder) is only justified if it outperforms a much simpler alternative — plain cosine similarity between final-layer activations alone. If the last layer already captures enough circuit structure to achieve comparable proxy ρ against patching, the trajectory machinery isn't adding value. Experiment: compute pairwise cosine similarity using only `h_8` (the final ResNet block output) and measure Spearman ρ against the existing patching ground truth. One day of work. If the full trajectory proxy substantially outperforms (ρ gap > 0.1), the multi-layer approach is justified. If not, the contribution needs to be reframed.

* **Uniform Weighting Ablation:**
The linear depth ramp (w_l = l / sum(1..L)) was motivated by the assumption that later layers encode more semantically consistent representations. The patching validation contradicted this — empirical causal influence is flat across all 8 layers, not increasing with depth. This means the depth ramp is an uncalibrated prior, not an empirically grounded design choice. Experiment: retrain Option A with uniform weighting (w_l = 1/L for all l) and compare proxy Spearman ρ against the current linear ramp. If uniform weighting achieves equal or better ρ, the paper's depth-weighting rationale needs to be revised to frame the ramp as a training heuristic rather than a reflection of circuit structure.

* **CIFAR-100 Replication:**
All results are currently on CIFAR-10 — 10 coarse classes, 32×32 images, relatively easy circuit discrimination. A reviewer will immediately ask whether the proxy holds on harder tasks. CIFAR-100 has 100 fine-grained classes with high within-class variation, making circuit discrimination harder and the 50/50 same-class split potentially more pronounced. Experiment: replicate the full pipeline (unified objective training, activation patching ground truth on a 1000-pair sample, proxy Spearman ρ) on CIFAR-100 with ResNet18. If ρ holds above 0.6, the scale concern is partially addressed. If it degrades significantly, that is an honest and important limitation to report.

* **UMAP Circuit Space Visualization:**
The 50/50 same-class split finding is difficult to communicate via tables alone. A UMAP projection of z-space, colored by class, would make the core finding visually immediate — same-class inputs splitting into sub-clusters, cross-class inputs grouping together due to shared computational routing, and a structure that does not map cleanly onto the label taxonomy. Compare side-by-side: baseline z-space UMAP (roughly random) vs. CTLS z-space UMAP (class-organized but with visible within-class sub-structure). The 3D version could add a depth axis to animate how trajectories resolve from ambiguous in early layers to class-specific in later layers. This is already buildable from existing z vectors and is probably the paper's most communicative figure.

* **RoPE-Style Layer Encoding in the Meta-Encoder:**
The current transformer meta-encoder (Option B) uses sinusoidal positional encodings to tell the transformer which token came from which layer. This encodes absolute position but doesn't build geometric relationships between layers into the representation itself. RoPE (Rotary Position Embedding) encodes layer position as a rotation in embedding space, meaning the dot product between two layer tokens automatically reflects their relative depth — adjacent layers are geometrically closer than distant layers. The deeper motivation is the "unraveling" property: if layer position is encoded as rotation angle, you could in principle project a z vector back into per-layer contributions by reversing the rotation, giving decomposed attribution over layers without needing Option A's fixed weighted sum. This would give Option B's per-input expressivity with Option A's interpretability — resolving the core tradeoff between the two variants. Experiment: implement RoPE positional encoding in the transformer meta-encoder, retrain, and compare proxy ρ against sinusoidal Option B. Additionally test whether the rotation-reversal attribution produces per-layer influence scores that correlate with the patching ground truth per-layer KL profiles.

* **Hebbian Co-Activation Regularization:**
InfoNCE is class-discriminative — it pulls same-class circuits together and pushes different-class circuits apart. This creates a risk of distorting circuits that are legitimately shared across classes, like curve detectors or texture circuits, which fire for cats, dogs, and birds simultaneously. The task loss provides some protection but is a blunt signal. A Hebbian regularization term would explicitly reward consistent co-activation of layer pairs across the full batch regardless of class label, reinforcing circuit components that are stably used across many inputs. This acts as a counterweight to InfoNCE's class-discriminative pressure and structurally protects shared cross-class circuits. Experiment: add a Hebbian term that rewards high cross-layer activation correlation across the batch, train with and without it, and use the patching ground truth to check whether shared cross-class circuit integrity (e.g., curve detector causal influence) is better preserved. The presence or absence of shared circuit degradation in the current model (measurable from per-layer patching KL profiles on cross-class pairs) determines whether this is urgent.

* **Circuit Economy as a Regularization Objective:**
The hypothesis: a network should use the minimum number of distinct computational pathways needed to correctly discriminate its outputs. Redundant circuits — multiple different routings that produce the same prediction — represent wasted capacity that may also represent memorization shortcuts that fail under distribution shift. This connects to the grokking literature, where the transition from memorization to generalization corresponds to a transition from redundant to efficient circuits. In z-space, circuit economy means penalizing within-class cluster variance — you want all same-class inputs to converge toward one canonical circuit (or a small family), not scatter across many. The soft-weighted positive pairs idea naturally implements a weak version of this. A stronger version would add an explicit within-class variance penalty on z in each batch. Experiment: add a variance penalty term on z within each class, compare proxy ρ and downstream generalization metrics (e.g., performance under distribution shift) against the version without it. If circuit economy correlates with generalization, this becomes a principled connection between interpretability and robustness.

* **Patching-Derived Positive Pairs (Offline Version):**
Rather than using z-similarity to define positive pairs dynamically during training (the soft-weighted approach), an alternative is to pre-compute CircuitSim from activation patching on a baseline model and use those as fixed positive pairs from the start of CTLS training. Pairs with CircuitSim above a threshold are positives; pairs below are left out of the loss entirely regardless of class label. This gives a cleaner, externally grounded positive pair definition that doesn't depend on the model's current z-space. The limitation is that it defines circuit structure from an unstructured baseline model, which may not be the right prior. Experiment: run patching on a standard ResNet18 trained without CTLS, use CircuitSim > 0.5 as the positive pair criterion, train CTLS with those pairs, and compare proxy ρ on the resulting model against class-label and soft-weighted variants. This experiment directly tests whether the quality of the positive pair definition during training determines the quality of the learned proxy.
